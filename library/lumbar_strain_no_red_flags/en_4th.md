DIAGNOSIS:
You have a low back strain. This means the muscles or ligaments in your lower back are sore or stretched.

WHAT WE FOUND:
Your exam today was reassuring. You can walk, and you do not have signs of a dangerous nerve problem.

WHAT TO DO AT HOME:
- Move around a little each day. Do not stay in bed all day. Short walks can help.
- Use ice for the first 1 to 2 days. Put it on for 15 to 20 minutes at a time. After that, try heat if it feels better.
- For the next few days, avoid heavy lifting, bending, and twisting. Go back to normal activity slowly as pain improves.
- Sleep in a position that feels comfortable. A pillow between your knees or under your knees may help.

MEDICATIONS:
- You can use acetaminophen for pain. Follow the bottle directions. Do not take more than the label says.
- Ibuprofen or naproxen may help pain. Take them with food. Do not use them if you have kidney disease, stomach bleeding, take blood thinners, or were told to avoid them.

RETURN TO ED IF:
- Come back now if you cannot control your pee or poop, or if your groin or butt feels numb.
- Come back now for new leg weakness, trouble walking, fever, a new injury, or pain that becomes much worse.

FOLLOW UP:
Call your primary care doctor's office or clinic. Say, "I was in the emergency department and was diagnosed with a low back strain. I need a follow-up visit within 1-2 weeks if pain is not improving."
